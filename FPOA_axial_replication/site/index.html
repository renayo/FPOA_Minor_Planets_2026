● The finding

Each named body's daily Google-News article count is normalised to unit length (so a heavily-covered body and a lightly-covered one carry equal total weight), then binned by its ecliptic longitude measured from the First Point of Aries (FPOA, the vernal equinox) into a single 360-point wave. We ask whether that wave repeats at the traditional aspect separations {30, 36, 40, 45, 60, 72, 90, 120, 180}°.

It does — weakly, and as one axis. The joint aspect autocorrelation runs several times its chance level against two count-preserving nulls; the nine aspect-lag autocorrelations collapse onto a single cosine at r = …; and that cosine's axis lands within about 2° of the equinoctial axis.

● Why the axis counts as pre-registered

The First Point of Aries is not a parameter fitted to these data. It is the zero of tropical ecliptic longitude — the vernal equinox — fixed by the coordinate system, the same way Greenwich fixes 0° terrestrial longitude. The ayanamsha, the tropical-to-sidereal offset, is likewise a defined constant. Both zodiacal conventions therefore name the same equinoctial axis in advance.

That determines which Bayes factor applies. The axis-free value penalises a search over all 360 directions and is modest. The fixed-axis value tests the pre-specified equinoctial axis and is the correct statistic here — and it is strong: up to about … against the unnamed-minor-planet control. The data then corroborate the pre-registration: the data-estimated axis (…) falls on the axis fixed in advance.

● What this is, and is not

This is an internally consistent, reproducible statistical regularity in one news index over one 290-day window, located on the tropical equinoctial axis. It is not a demonstrated physical or causal mechanism, and the effect is weak when the axis is treated as unknown. The signal is one coherent axial cosine — best reported as a single weak axis, not as nine independent aspects.
